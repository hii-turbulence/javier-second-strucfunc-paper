% Abstract of the paper
\begin{abstract}
  % Context
  Turbulence plays a critical role in the evolution of \hii\ regions, yet recovering its statistical properties from observations remains challenging, especially under low signal-to-noise conditions.
  % What we did
  We investigate whether high spatial resolution but low spectra integral field spectroscopic observations from VLT MUSE of the Orion Nebula (M~42) can recover the same turbulent velocity as derived from previously analyzed observations at high spectral resolution but lower spatial resolution.
  % How we did it
  We analyze the centroid velocity maps by computing the second-order structure function of the velocity fluctuations in the plane-of-sky. We apply a masking procedure based on surface brightness and systematically test binning to optimize the trade-off between instrumental noise and spatial resolution. A model is then fitted to the observed structure functions to extract the key turbulent parameters, including the correlation length $r_0$, slope $m$, and noise level.
  % What we found
  We find that downsampling significantly improves both the reliability and computational efficiency of the structure function up to a limit set by the ratio between the bin size and the correlation length (\(\text{Bin size} / r_0 \sim 0.1\)). 
  At the same time, the fitted model alone is able to recover the turbulent parameters across all binning levels, including low signal-to-noise conditions. 
  Differences in the large-scale behavior among emission lines of different ionization potential are observed, revealing line-dependent turbulence signatures associated with the geometric and ionization structure of the nebula. 
  Our methodology enables a consistent recovery of turbulent parameters under varying observational conditions. 
  Comparison with KPNO echelle observations yields good overall agreement in the turbulent parameters.
  % Take-away statement
  We show that, despite their relatively poor velocity resolution, our methodology can turn MUSE observations into a useful tool for studying turbulent velocity, even at the subsonic velocity dispersion typical of compact H~II regions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

%Intro first para (what we are studying)
The process of star formation begins with the gravitational collapse of molecular clouds, followed by fragmentation into dense cores where stars form in clusters \citep{2003RPPh...66.1651L, 2007ARA&A..45..565M}. 
Massive O- and B-type stars emerge within these stellar groups, and their intense ionizing radiation and stellar winds inject substantial energy and momentum into the surrounding gas, profoundly influencing the structure and dynamics of the the interstellar medium (ISM) \citep{2006ApJ...647..397M,2024RMxAC..58....8A}.

Observations of both molecular and ionized gas reveal complex velocity and density structures that deviate from simple motions,e.g., like radial expansion \citep{Jaupart:2022i,2025ApJ...986..159R}. 
Instead, these motions exhibit complex behavior that can be interpreted as turbulence. 
In this context, \hii\ regions—especially nearby ones—offer ideal laboratories for studying turbulent dynamics in the ionized ISM \citep{1987ApJ...317..686O,castaneda1988,Mivi1995,2026A&A...707A.339R}.

Turbulence in \hii\ regions is typically first characterized through the amplitude of velocity fluctuations. This can be studied using velocity dispersion along the line-of-sight (LOS) or through the variance of centroid velocities projected onto the plane-of-sky (POS) \citep{1988A&A...201..199A,arthur2016turbulence}. 
Statistical tools, such as the second-order structure function, enable the recovery of turbulence signatures in the POS such as the power-law $m$ and correlation length \(r_0\), and allow comparisons with theoretical models such as the Kolmogorov theory of incompressible turbulence \citep{von1951methode, castaneda1988, arthur2016turbulence, lagrois2011}.

However, recovering these turbulent parameters from observational data is not straightforward. 
Since the structure function characterizes how velocity fluctuations vary with spatial separation, it is essential that the measured scales reflect genuine turbulent motions rather than instrumental or environmental effects.
%Since the turbulent cascade is inherently scale-dependent, it is essential that the measured spatial scales accurately reflect genuine turbulent velocity fluctuations, free from contamination by instrumental or environmental effects.

In practice, this condition is difficult to satisfy. 
Ground-based observations are affected by atmospheric seeing, while the smallest measurable velocity fluctuations are limited by the instrument’s spectral resolution and sensitivity. 
In addition, noise and other systematic effects can distort the observed velocity field. 
Furthermore, \hii\ regions are dynamic environments that exhibit large-scale motions not directly related to turbulence, which must be carefully disentangled when interpreting the data \citep{1979ApJ...233...85B,Mivi1995, lagrois2011}.

The Orion Nebula (M~42), located at a distance of 440 pc \citetext{\SI{1}{\arcsecond} = \SI{0.002}{pc}; \citealp{2008AJ....136.1566O}}, is the closest massive star-forming region and one of the most studied \hii\ regions. 
Ionized by the O7~V star $\theta^1$~Ori~C, the nebula exhibits complex physical and kinematic structures, including a rich population of young stars, stellar outflows, and Herbig–Haro objects \citep{1993ApJ...410..696O,2001ARA&A..39...99O,Garcia-Diaz:2007a,2009AJ....137..367O,2019MNRAS.486.3423H,2021MNRAS.502.1703M}. 
Its proximity and brightness make it a prime target for studies using spectroscopic observations \citep{Garcia-Diaz:2008a,2015A&A...582A.114W,2016ApJ...819..136A}.

Several studies have investigated turbulence in the Orion Nebula using statistical tools such as the second-order structure function \citep{von1951methode,munch1958internal,castaneda1988,1992ApJ...387..229O,arthur2016turbulence,2016MNRAS.455.4057M,2019MNRAS.483..704A}. 
However, these analyses have led to significantly different conclusions regarding the nature of the turbulent cascade. 
For example, \citet{arthur2016turbulence}, using Kitt Peak National Observatory (KPNO) echelle observations, reported clear power-law behavior consistent with a turbulent cascade, while \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}, analyzing Very Large Telescope (VLT) Multi Unit Spectroscopic Explorer (MUSE) data, found little evidence of such structure. 
More recent studies using the same VLT MUSE observations \citep{2019MNRAS.483..704A} have obtained intermediate results.

%This results is in contrast to what \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M} obtained by analyzing MUSE observations, where the a lack of velocity fluctuations structure was reported.
%As noted by \citet{arthur2016turbulence}, the absence of turbulent cascade in \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M} is probably because of the heavy noise-to-signal ratio which comes from observations.

These discrepancies are likely driven by differences in methodology, data quality, and the treatment of observational effects such as noise and spatial resolution \citep{garciav23}. 
In particular, instrumental noise can obscure small-scale velocity fluctuations, making the recovery of the turbulent cascade highly sensitive to the analysis technique. This raises a fundamental question: how can the turbulent structure function be reliably recovered from noisy integral field spectroscopic observations? 

More generally, the lack of a standardized methodology for extracting turbulent parameters from observational data makes direct comparisons between studies difficult. 
This fragmentation poses a major challenge for turbulence research, especially in the absence of a complete astrophysical turbulent theory, where observational constraints play a central role.


%(how we are studying it)
%A major challenge in analyzing turbulence from observational data lies in recovering the intrinsic turbulent behavior while minimizing the effects of instrumental noise, seeing, and resolution limits.

In \citet{garciav23}, we introduced a heuristic model for the second-order structure function and applied it to \hii\ regions of varying sizes. 
This approach provided a coherent methodology that not only enabled the recovery of turbulent parameters, but also allowed for meaningful comparisons between different regions.

In this work, we address these issues by implementing a consistent methodology to recover the turbulent structure function from noisy observations. 
By combining masking, controlled binning, and model fitting, we aim to reliably extract the turbulent parameters and provide a framework for meaningful comparison between different datasets.
We also extend its use to new observational data and address how to mitigate nuisance parameters that commonly affect the measurement of turbulence in ionized gas.

In Section~\ref{sec:observations}, we describe the VLT MUSE integral-field spectroscopic observations of the Huygens region in the Orion Nebula, which we use as a concrete example to analyze the turbulent velocity field across multiple emission lines: \halpha, \oiii, \n, \siii, and \ariii.
In Section~\ref{sec:met}, we combine a systematic selection procedure with a downsampling strategy to recover the intrinsic second-order structure function of the velocity fluctuations. 
The resulting turbulent parameters are then derived and analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:results}.
In Section~\ref{sec:discussion}, we examine how the recovered turbulent parameters vary between emission lines and datasets, and discuss their implications for the turbulent structure of ionized gas. Particular attention is given to the extent to which the turbulent cascade can be recovered from noisy observational data.
Finally, Section~\ref{sec:summary} summarizes our results.

Figure~\ref{fig:velocity_maps_shared_cbar_fixed} shows the centroid velocity maps, \(V_c\), for each emission line in our sample.

The overall amplitude of the plane-of-sky velocity fluctuations can be characterized by a dispersion, \(\sigma\pos\), defined as:
%
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sig-pos}
  \sigma^2\pos =
  \left\langle 
  \bigl[ V_c (\xx_i) -\langle V_ c\rangle  \bigr]^2
  \right \rangle ,
\end{equation}
where the average is performed over all observed points \(i\)
in a given map.
Note that \(\sigma\pos\) is also the RMS width of
the probability density function (PDF) of \(V_c\).
The PDFs for all emission lines analyzed in this work are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pdfs}.

 \textcolor{red}{The PDFs are shown after subtracting the mean velocity from each map, centering all distributions at \SI{0}{km\,s^{-1}}. 
They represent the amplitude distribution of the plane-of-sky velocity fluctuations and exhibit approximately Gaussian profiles for all emission lines. 
This behavior is expected after removing high-velocity features associated with Herbig-Haro objects and proplyds, together with low signal-to-noise pixels and regions contaminated by bright stars, resulting in more symmetric velocity distributions.
All PDFs span a similar velocity range, approximately from \SI{-10}{km\,s^{-1}} to \SI{10}{km\,s^{-1}} which display broadly similar kinematic behavior.
The \oiii\ emission line presents a small tail toward positive values which maps a southwest region in Figure~\ref{fig:velocity_maps_shared_cbar_fixed}. 
The broader distribution observed in the \ariii\ emission line is likely dominated by observational noise due to the intrinsic faintness of the line, whereas the narrower distribution of \halpha\ reflects its higher signal-to-noise ratio.}

\textcolor{red}{Although the PDFs characterize the overall amplitude of the velocity fluctuations through \(\sigma\pos\), they do not contain information about the spatial correlations of the velocity field. 
Therefore, a correlation analysis, such as the second-order structure function, is required to characterize the scale dependence of the turbulent fluctuations.}

\subsection{The binning procedure}\label{sec:binning procedure}

To mitigate the impact of instrumental noise while preserving the underlying turbulent signal, we apply a spatial binning procedure to the velocity maps using the \texttt{tetrablok} Python package\footnote{\url{https://github.com/will-henney/tetrabloks}} \citep{2018MNRAS.479.3909G}. 
This approach explicitly addresses the trade-off between noise reduction and spatial resolution in the recovery of the turbulent structure function.

The binning is performed by re-sampling the original data onto progressively coarser grids using binning factors of \(2^1 = 2\), \(2^2 = 4\), \(2^3 = 8\), and \(2^4 = 16\) applied in each spatial direction. 
At each binning level, the velocity field is averaged over non-overlapping square blocks of size \(N \times N\) pixels (e.g., \(2 \times 2\), \(4 \times 4\), \(8 \times 8\), and \(16 \times 16\)), producing a lower-resolution map.
While this process reduces small-scale noise fluctuations and improves computational efficiency, it also smooths the velocity field, potentially limiting the recovery of the fluctuations of the smallest spatial scales.

Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_bins_comparison} shows as an example of the binned velocity maps for the \halpha\ emission line.
The original map has a size of \(1 475 \times1765 \ \text{pixels}\) (see Fig.~\ref{fig:H_I-6563_maps}) which is reduced to a size of \(91 \times 109 \ \text{pixels}\) through a series of four iterations.
Each iteration and the resulting velocity map is shown in the different panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:Ha_bins_comparison} for the \halpha\ emission line.

\subsection{Surface brightness masking and weighted analysis}\label{sec:sb masking}


A common approach to improve the signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) in observational data is to exclude pixels with low surface brightness. 
While this procedure increases the S/N, it introduces a bias by preferentially selecting brighter regions and discarding information from fainter parts of the map. 
As a result, there is no guarantee that the underlying structure of the velocity fluctuations is faithfully recovered.
 
Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_sb_mask_map} illustrates the effect of this procedure, showing the spatial coverage obtained after normalizing the surface brightness in the \halpha\ emission line and removing all pixels below a more aggressive brightness threshold of \(0.07\).
As a consequence, velocity structures associated with the extended low surface-brightness emission, visible in the right-hand panel of Figure~\ref{fig:H_I-6563_maps}, are largely discarded, restricting the analysis to the brightest central region of the nebula.

To reduce the arbitrariness associated with a strict surface-brightness threshold, we also consider a intensity-weighted structure function (see equation \eqref{eq:Br_w} in Section~\ref{sec:second-order-struct}). 
This approach enhances the contribution of brighter pixels while preserving information from low surface-brightness regions, providing a more continuous treatment of the data.

Both methods are evaluated in comparison with the binning procedure described in the \S~\ref{sec:binning procedure}, which directly addresses the trade-off between noise reduction and spatial resolution.

\subsection{The second-order structure function}
\label{sec:second-order-struct}

 
After the binning and masking of the maps we apply the second-order structure function, \(B(r)\), which is a function of the scalar separation or lag, \(r\),
between two points on the plane of the sky:
%
\newcommand\Abs[1]{\vert #1\vert}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Br}
  B(r) = \left\langle 
  \bigl[
  V_{c}(\xx_j) - V_{c}(\xx_i)
  \bigr]^{2} \right \rangle_{\Abs{\xx_j - \xx_i\!} \ \approx \ r} \ .
\end{equation}

The averaging is performed over all pairs of points
\((i, j)\)
whose scalar separation \(\Abs{\xx_j - \xx_i}\) is close to \(r\),
irrespective of the orientation of the separation vector.
In practice, we achieve this by binning the separations with a constant
logarithmic width of \SI{0.05}{dex}.

We also use an intensity-weighted structure function, defined as:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Br_w}
  B(r) =\left\langle \frac{I(\xx_j)I(\xx_i)}{\langle I(\xx_j)I(\xx_i) \rangle } 
  \bigl[
  V_{c}(\xx_j) - V_{c}(\xx_i)
  \bigr]^{2}  \right \rangle_{\Abs{\xx_j - \xx_i\!} \ \approx \ r} \ .
\end{equation}
%
where \(I(\xx_j)\) and \(I(\xx_i)\) are the intensities at each pair of pixels.
In this formulation, brighter structures are emphasized, while fainter regions contribute less to the structure function, which in turn helps to mitigate the influence of noise \citep{arthur2016turbulence}.

To interpret the observed structure function and extract the underlying turbulent parameters, we adopt a parametric model that accounts for both intrinsic turbulence and observational effects \citep{garciav23}. 
In this framework, the observed structure function is modeled by combining the intrinsic structure function with the effects of atmospheric seeing, \(S(r)\), and a constant noise contribution, \(B\noise\), yielding the corrected model:
%
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{B}\model(r) = B\model(r) \,  S(r) + B\noise
  \label{eq:sf-functional}
\end{equation}
where \(B\model(r)\) represents the intrinsic (noise- and seeing-free) structure function, which we model as:
%
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal}
  B\model(r) = 2\sigma^2\pos \left[
    1 - 2^{- \left( r/r_0 \right)^m} \ .
  \right]
\end{equation}
%
Here, \(r_0\) is the correlation length, the characteristic length of the velocity fluctuations, and \(m\) is the is the power-law index, which characterize the slope of the turbulent cascade.

In equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal}, the seeing \(S(r)\) is given by:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ffs}
  S(r) =
  \left[ \frac{1}{1 + 1.25 s_0 / r_0} \right]
  \left[ \frac{1}{1 + (2.6 s_0 / r)^{1.5}} \right]
  ,
\end{equation}
where \(s_0\) is the RMS width of the seeing profile\footnote{%
  Note that the full-width half maximum (FWHM) seeing width is
  \(2 (2 \ln 2)^{1/2} s_0 \approx 2.35 s_0\).
}.

The observed structure function, \(B\obs(r)\), is computed using equation~\eqref{eq:Br}. 
The uncertainties in \(B\obs(r)\) are assumed to be dominated by systematic effects rather than random noise. Consequently, we assign an uncertainty to each bin equal to a constant fraction (1–3\%) of the observed value (see column~3 of Table~\ref{tab:fitting-parameters}).

Model fitting is performed using a non-linear weighted least-squares approach based on the Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm \citep{More:1978a}, as implemented in the \texttt{lmfit} Python library \citep{newville_matthew_2014_11813}. 
The posterior distributions of the model parameters are estimated using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) ensemble sampling \citep{2010CAMCS...5...65G}, implemented in the \texttt{emcee} Python library \citep{2013PASP..125..306F}.  
Table~\ref{tab:parameter-ranges} lists the uniform prior distributions assumed for each parameter.

To assess the reliability of the fits, we consider the turbulent parameters to be reliably recovered when a power-law regime is identified and the derived parameters are well constrained. For this, we require statistical consistency between the model and the data, quantified by a reduced chi-square value \(\chi^2 \lesssim 1\) (see column~7 of Table~\ref{tab:fitting-parameters}). 
We also verify that the posterior distributions of the fitted parameters are well-behaved, without significant asymmetries or multimodalities, within the 95\% confidence interval.

A detailed description of our structure function methodology can be found in \citet{garciav23}. 
In this work, we focus on its application to observational data processed through binning and masking, with the aim of recovering the underlying turbulent cascade.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

In this section we present the turbulent parameters and their confidence intervals obtained by fitting equation~\eqref{eq:sf-functional} to the observed structure function, \(B\obs(r)\). 

The results are analyzed to assess how observational effects, such as noise, spatial resolution, and field of view, as well as different analysis methods, impact the recovery of the structure function and the derived parameters.

\subsection{Noise-resolution trade off}\label{sec:noise-resolution trade off}


We first present the results for the \halpha\ and \ariii\ emission lines, which correspond to the brightest and faintest lines in our sample, respectively. 
These two cases illustrate the effects of masking and binning on the structure function under different signal-to-noise conditions. 
The same analysis is applied to the remaining emission lines, with results presented in the Appendix~\ref{apex:multiple_lines_results}.

\subsubsection{Representative Case Study for the \halpha\ line}\label{sec:Representative case study}


% Present Brs for Halpha
The solid markers of different colors in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison} indicate the results of applying equation~\eqref{eq:Br} to the non-binned \halpha\ map shown in Figure~\ref{fig:velocity_maps_shared_cbar_fixed}, and the corresponding binned maps in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_bins_comparison}.
The dotted curves correspond to the heuristic model described in equation~\eqref{eq:sf-functional}, while the dashed curves represent the ideal model given in equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal}.  
For each binning level the size of the pixel in parsecs is \num{0.0004}, \num{0.0008}, \num{0.0016}, \num{0.0032} and \(0.00064\).

% Present trubulent parameters for Halpha
For the \halpha\ emission we were able to fit the model to all the observed structure functions. Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE_Ha} lists the best-fit values for the model parameters with 95\% credibility range. The first column corresponds to the value for the binning size used in each map before computing the structure function.
The nuisance values like the noise and seeing (in RMS and FWHM) are given in columns three, four and eleven.
The turbulent parameters are listed in columns two, five and seven.
Finally, columns eight, nine and ten list the quotients \(L/r_0\) ($L$ being the observational box length), \(s_0/r_0\) and \(\text{Bin size} / r_0\), which are used for a sanity check of the results.

% Halpha Discussion small scales
For the non-binned, \halpha\ map (Bin~0), a power-law trend is present, but the structure at small scales is significantly affected by instrumental noise, with a derived value of \SI{2.61}{km^{2}.s^{-2}}.  
The structure functions derived from the binned maps (Bin~1–4) are also shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison}.  
As the bin size increases, from \num{2} to \num{16}, the small-scale structure of the velocity field becomes progressively clearer, as evidenced by the emergence of a well-defined power-law behavior and a reduction of the instrumental noise to a value of \SI{0.39}{km^{2}.s^{-2}}.  

% Noise reduction
Although binning smooths the velocity field, it effectively suppresses small-scale noise, allowing the underlying turbulent structure to be recovered. 
However, this improvement reaches a limit, beyond which increasing the bin size does not lead to a significant noise reduction and begins to remove physically relevant small-scale information as seen in Figure~\ref{fig:Orion_sf_1}.
This behavior is reflected in the derived noise values for the largest binning factors (\num{8} and \num{16}), which converge to similar levels of \SI{\sim 0.40}{km^{2}.s^{-2}} and, indicating that further binning does not lead to a significant reduction in instrumental noise.

% Increse in seeing
As for the seeing (FWHM) parameter, in Figure~\ref{fig:Orion_sf_1} we observe a systematic increase in the derived values with increasing bin size. 
For the binned samples, the fitted parameter \(s_0\) increases from \SI{0.76}{arcsec} to \SI{3.40}{arcsec}. 
Because all maps originate from the same MUSE observations, this trend cannot be attributed to variations in the atmospheric seeing. 
Rather, the increase in \(s_0\) reflects the additional spatial smoothing introduced by binning, which progressively suppresses small-scale velocity fluctuations. 
For comparison, \citet{2015A&A...582A.114W} report DIMM (Differential Image Motion Monitor) seeing values between \SI{0.67}{arcsec} and \SI{1.25}{arcsec} during the observations.

%Seeing-noise trade off
This trend reflects the intrinsic trade-off of the binning procedure: while the reduction of noise enables the recovery of the structure function from noisy observations, it simultaneously smooths the velocity field, increasing the apparent blurring and suppressing small-scale fluctuations. 
This effect is comparable to the blurring introduced by atmospheric seeing in ground-based observations.

Therefore, there exists a practical limit to the binning procedure, beyond which small-scale fluctuations can no longer be reliably recovered and the derived turbulent parameters may become biased due to the loss of physical information. 
To quantify this limit, we consider the ratio between the bin size and the correlation length, \(\text{Bin size} / r_0\). 
We find that when this quantity is of the order of \num{0.1}, further binning does not lead to a significant reduction in noise and the seeing increases abruptly.

% Halpha Large scales
At large spatial scales, the \halpha\ structure function shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison} exhibits a downward hook, which reflects a gradient where the amplitude of the velocity fluctuations decreases toward the edges of the observational field. 
This behavior is consistently observed across all binning levels.

% Model recovering for all maps
An important result shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison} is that the fitting procedure of the equation~\eqref{eq:sf-functional}, is able to recover the turbulent parameters even at low signal-to-noise ratios. 
This is illustrated by the dashed lines, which represent the ideal model given by equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal} and closely match the observed structure functions across different binning levels. 

This demonstrates that our methodology, based on fitting a heuristic model to the observational data, is sufficient to recover the turbulent parameters of the velocity field. 
In practice, this implies that either the non-binned maps can be used, at the expense of increased computational cost, or binned maps can be employed within the constraint imposed by the ratio \(\text{Bin size} / r_0\), without compromising the recovery of the turbulent cascade. 
This result extends the applicability of the method to datasets with low signal-to-noise ratios or limited spatial resolution.

\subsubsection{Representative Case Study for the \ariii\ emission line}\label{sec:Representative case study Ar}

% [Ar III] Discussion
Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE_Ar} presents the best-fit model parameters and their corresponding confidence intervals for the \ariii\ emission line, where the columns represent the same quantities as in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE_Ha}. The associated structure functions are shown in the right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison}.

As discussed for the \halpha\ emission, the heuristic model given by equation~\eqref{eq:sf-functional} provides a good fit to all observed structure functions. 
This result demonstrates that the combined use of binning and model fitting allows the turbulent cascade to be reliably recovered even in low signal-to-noise conditions. 
In particular, it highlights the robustness of the methodology when applied to fainter emission lines, where noise effects are more significant.

The \ariii\ emission exhibits a similar trend to that observed for \halpha, in which the structure of the velocity fluctuations can be recovered either by fitting the model to the non-binned map or by applying spatial binning. 
Since this line is intrinsically fainter, the binning procedure is particularly effective at recovering the velocity structure, reducing the instrumental noise from \SI{10.8}{km^{2}.s^{-2}} to \SI{1}{km^{2}.s^{-2}}.

One key difference for the \ariii\ emission, compared to \halpha\ in our analysis, is the behavior of the inferred seeing parameter \(s_0\), which decreases from the non-binned map and reaches a minimum value of \SI{1.4}{arcsec} at intermediate binning levels (bin~4). 
We attribute this behavior to the way the model responds to different noise regimes. For the non-binned (bin~0) and lightly binned (bin~1) maps, the signal-to-noise ratio is low due to the faintness of the line, and the observed structure function does not exhibit a clear power-law behavior, as it is dominated by noise. 
In this regime, the fitting procedure compensates by adopting relatively large values of \(s_0\), effectively reproducing the apparent flattening of the structure function at small separations.

As the noise is reduced through binning (e.g., bin~2), the underlying power-law behavior becomes more clearly defined, allowing the model to recover smaller and more physically meaningful values of \(s_0\), consistent with the presence of resolved small-scale velocity fluctuations.
At larger binning factors, however, the averaging process begins to smooth out these small-scale fluctuations, leading again to an increase in the inferred \(s_0\). 
In this regime, the rise in \(s_0\) reflects the loss of intrinsic small-scale structure due to the effective blurring introduced by the binning procedure.

Regarding the large-scale behavior of the \ariii\ structure functions, we observe a transition from a periodic, oscillatory pattern in the non-binned map to a radial-gradient behavior in the binned maps, similar to that found for \halpha.  
This suggests that the oscillatory pattern is likely driven by the low signal-to-noise ratio in the non-binned data, while the smoothing introduced by binning recovers the intrinsic large-scale velocity structure of the emission line.

As in the case of \halpha, the observed structure functions show good overall agreement with the model described by equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal} across all binned samples.
This behavior further supports the robustness of the methodology in recovering the turbulent structure across different emission lines, even under varying signal-to-noise conditions.

The left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:fit_results_01} shows the observed structure functions (bin~0) for the \halpha\ and \ariii\ emission lines, together with the best-fit model and the corresponding 95\% credibility intervals for the parameters. 
The right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:fit_results_01} presents the pairwise correlations of the posterior distributions of the model parameters, obtained using the \texttt{corner} Python library \citep{2017ascl.soft02002F}. 

Figure~\ref{fig:Orion_sf_1} extends this analysis to all binned samples of \halpha, illustrating the progressive recovery of small-scale fluctuations with increasing bin size, as well as the consistency of the best-fit model and parameter constraints across different resolutions.

\subsection{Comparison between binning, masking and the weighted structure function}

In this section, we present the results of the weighted structure function and compare them with those obtained from surface-brightness masking. 
To address the limitations of masking, which removes low-intensity regions and may bias the velocity field, we explore a weighted formulation of the structure function. 
This approach retains contributions from low surface-brightness regions while reducing the dependence on an arbitrary threshold.

Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_w_comparison} shows the weighted structure function and the structure function derived from the masked velocity map. 
For comparison, the structure function of the non-binned map (\(2^0 = 1\)) is also included. 
The best-fit parameters and their corresponding confidence intervals are presented in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSEw}.

From Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSEw}, we recover the same general behavior observed previously: the fitting procedure is able to extract the turbulent parameters from the data. 
However, in the absence of binning, there is no significant reduction in the instrumental noise, which remains at an average value of \SI{2.5}{km^{2}.s^{-2}} for these samples. 
%Regarding the seeing, both the weighted analysis and the masking approach lead to higher inferred values. 
%For the non-binned and the weighted structure function, the seeing is \SI{0.8}{arcsec}, while for the surface brightness mask it increase to \SI{1}{arcsec}.

An interesting result of this analysis is that, as noted earlier, fitting the heuristic model to noisy maps alone is sufficient to recover the turbulent parameters. 
Here we confirm this behavior, with differences arising primarily in the plane-of-sky velocity dispersion, \(\sigma\pos\), and the correlation length, \(r_0\), depending on the method applied.

In particular, we find a significant difference in \(\sigma\pos\) between the masked map, with a value of \SI{13}{km^{2}.s^{-2}}, and the weighted and non-masked samples, which yield values of \SI{\sim 9}{km^{2}.s^{-2}}. 
This difference arises because the masking procedure emphasizes the inner regions of the nebula while discarding the outer parts. 
Given the presence of a radial gradient in the nebula—where velocity fluctuations decrease toward the outer regions—this selection leads to an increase in the measured \(\sigma\pos\).

For the correlation length, \(r_0\), the main difference is observed in the weighted structure function, which yields values in the range \SI{0.041}{pc} to \SI{0.047}{pc}, compared to the other samples. 
This reduction in \(r_0\) may be attributed to the smaller spatial scales probed when the analysis emphasizes brighter pixels, which are concentrated in a more confined region (see Figure~\ref{fig:H_I-6563_maps}). 
In contrast, the other samples include velocity fluctuations over a more extended area, leading to larger inferred correlation lengths.
As for the power-law index, all the samples give a mean value of \num{1.1}.

We also find that, for the ideal model, Equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal}, the weighted and masked cases show better agreement for small scales, while the weighted and non-binned model show better agreement for large scales. 
This highlights the dependence of the recovered turbulent parameters on the adopted methodology, emphasizing the need for a consistent and physically motivated approach when interpreting structure function results.

Appendix~\ref{apex:weigthed_br_results} shows the structure functions and corner plots (Figure~\ref{fig:fit_results_w}) for the \halpha\  emission line for the weighted and masked analysis and their fitting parameters in Table~\ref{tab:fitting-parameters-w}.

The results presented above demonstrate that the recovery of the turbulent structure function, and consequently the inferred turbulent parameters, is strongly dependent on the treatment of observational effects such as noise, spatial resolution, and surface-brightness selection. 
While binning, masking, and weighted approaches all allow for the extraction of turbulent parameters, they lead to systematic differences in the recovered values, particularly in \(\sigma\pos\) and \(r_0\). 
These differences reflect not only the impact of instrumental limitations, but also the sensitivity of the structure function to the spatial regions sampled and the methodology employed.

Given these considerations, a direct comparison with previous studies requires careful evaluation of both the data quality and the analysis techniques used. 
In the following section, we compare our results with those reported in the literature, focusing on how methodological differences influence the interpretation of turbulence in the Orion Nebula.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

For the discussion of the turbulent parameters and their comparison with previous results, we adopt the analysis corresponding to a binning factor which gives us the lower value of the seeing \(s_0\), as this configuration provides the best compromise between noise reduction and spatial resolution.
The resulting turbulent parameters and their confidence intervals are listed in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE}. 
For completeness, Appendix~\ref{apex:multiple_lines_results} presents the second-order structure functions and their corresponding corner plots for all emission lines analyzed in this work.

\subsection{Comparison with previous works}\label{sec:comparion previous works}

%Other recent turbulent works on Orion
Our results are compared with previous studies that have computed the structure function for the Orion Nebula. 
Among works based on VLT MUSE observations are \citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A}, and \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}. 
In contrast, \citet{arthur2016turbulence} derived the structure function using echelle observations from the Kitt Peak National Observatory (KPNO) for \halpha, \oiii, and \n, complemented by data from the Observatorio Astronómico Nacional at San Pedro Mártir (OAN-SPM) for the \sii\ line.

\textcolor{red}{Before comparing the derived turbulent parameters, it is important to note that the studies considered here employ different procedures for selecting the velocity samples used in the structure-function analysis.
\citet{arthur2016turbulence} constructed mask from pixels with extreme values of the centroid identified through consideration of their PDFs. 
These pixels correspond to high-velocity features, such as HH 201, 202, 203, 204, and 529, and also to numerical artifacts introduced during the construction of the velocity maps from the individual slit observations.
\citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M} applied an intensity-based selection criterion for a mask, excluding pixels with \oi\ surface brightness below \SI{\sim 1e-16}{erg.s^{-1}.cm^{-2}.pixel^{-1}}. They then computed the second-order structure function for both the masked and unmasked velocity maps, while also considering the influence of high-velocity objects.
\citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A} compute the second-order structure function to the raw maps and filtered maps based on selection of specific intervals in the PDFs as: \(-9\sigma/5\sigma\), \(-1\sigma/5\sigma\), \(-9\sigma/-1\sigma\) and \(-1\sigma/1\sigma\). }

For direct comparison, Table~\ref{tab:results_KPNO} lists the turbulent parameters obtained by applying our methodology to the KPNO data and in Appendix~\ref{apex:kpno_structure_function} we present the structure functions and their corresponding corner plots and the fitting parameters in Table~\ref{tab:fitting-parameters-KPNO}. 
For details about the KPNO observations, we refer the reader to Section~2 of \citet{arthur2016turbulence}. 

\subsubsection{Plane-of-sky velocity dispersion, \(\sigma\pos\)}\label{sec:comparion previous works sigma}

%Sigma2 discussion
Since the KPNO observations cover an area of \(3' \times 5'\), while the VLT MUSE observations cover \(6' \times 5'\) (both centered on $\theta^1$~Orionis~C), one key difference seen in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE} and Table~\ref{tab:results_KPNO} is the larger value of \(\sigma\pos\) measured for the \halpha\ and \oiii\ emission lines in the KPNO data. 
Because the \halpha\ emission traces a larger volume of the nebula, whereas the \oiii\ emission originates primarily from the inner regions, the observed difference is likely related to the spatial extent of the observations. 
The larger MUSE field of view samples both the central high-amplitude fluctuations and the more quiescent outer regions, where the amplitude of the centroid velocity fluctuations is lower. Consequently, the measured value of \(\sigma\pos^2\) differs from that obtained using the smaller KPNO field of view.
%Because the \halpha\ emission traces a large volume of the nebula, whereas the \oiii\ emission originates from the inner regions, this difference likely reflects the variation in the field of view. 
%In particular, the larger MUSE field of view captures a broader radial velocity gradient, with velocity fluctuations decreasing toward the outer regions of the nebula. 

%This interpretation is supported by the value of \(\sigma\pos\) for the \n\ emission line, which is approximately \SI{\sim 8.5}{km\,s^{-1}} as this line traces the outer layers of the nebula.

\subsubsection{Power-law index, \(m\)}\label{sec:comparion previous works power-law}

%Power-law discussion
For the power-law index \(m\), the values derived from the VLT MUSE and KPNO datasets are broadly consistent. 
As reported in \citet{arthur2016turbulence}, the more pronounced steepening below \SI{2}{arcsec} (\SI{\sim0.002}{pc}) in the KPNO observations arises from the spatial resolution of the data, corresponding to the regime where seeing effects and the inter-slit separation become significant (See section~3.2.1 of their work). 
This behavior is reflected in the fitting of our model, which yields slightly higher values of \(m\) for the KPNO data. 
This can be seen in the observed structure functions (compare solid blue markers in Figure~\ref{fig:fit_results_01} and Figure~\ref{fig:fit_results_04}), which lie below the ideal model (green curves in the figures), in contrast to the VLT MUSE observations which lie above the model.

Values reported by \citet{arthur2016turbulence} are \(m>1\) for \halpha\ and \oiii\ emission lines and \(m\sim1\) for \n\ and \sii\ emission lines which matches our results Table~\ref{tab:results_KPNO}.
The power-law indices were determined through fitting a line between scales of \(8 < r < 22 \ \text{arcsec}\) (\(0.016 < r < 0.044 \ \text{pc}\)) for all emission lines except \sii\ which was obtained for scales  \(7 < r < 32 \ \text{arcsec}\) (\(0.014 < r < 0.064 \ \text{pc}\)).

A further comparison can be made with the results of \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}. 
%Random sampling for the structure function...
In their analysis, the power-law index was estimated by fitting a straight line to the observed structure function over specific ranges of spatial separations. 
For the \halpha\ emission line, they obtained a slope of \num{0.41} for separations in the range \(0.039 < r < 0.19\ \text{pc}\), while for the \oiii\ emission line they reported a value of \num{0.22} over separations of \(0.025 < r < 0.14\ \text{pc}\).
They argue that the structure function cannot be reliably recovered due to the low signal-to-noise ratio. 
However, as demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:met}, the recovery of the structure function is strongly dependent on the analysis methodology, and appropriate treatment of noise and spatial resolution allows the turbulent signal to be recovered even in low signal-to-noise conditions.

Another study that uses the same VLT MUSE observations to compute the second-order structure function is \citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A}, who presents the second-order structure function for the \halpha\ emission line using several masking strategies based on asymmetric cutoffs defined from the standard deviation of the radial velocity map. 
The most direct comparison with our results corresponds to what they call "filter~4", where the original velocity map is masked by retaining values within the range \(-\sigma\pos, +\sigma\pos\).
As opposed to \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}, the structure function is computed for the entire map instead of using random samples as in \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}.

\subsubsection{Correlation length, \(r_0\)}\label{sec:comparion previous works correlation length}

%Correlation length discussion
As mentioned earlier, the absence of a standardized methodology for determining the power-law index complicates direct comparisons between different studies. 
In this context, the model adopted in this work provides a consistent framework for fitting observed structure functions and extracting the parameters that describe the turbulent velocity fluctuations, offering a robust basis for future inter-dataset comparisons.

An additional advantage of our methodology is that the model fitting also allows us to recover the correlation length \(r_0\), which represents a characteristic scale of the turbulent structure. 
In this case, neither \citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A}, \citet{arthur2016turbulence} nor \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M} report values for this parameter, preventing a direct comparison with their results. 

For the \halpha\ and \oiii\ emission lines, both the VLT MUSE and KPNO observations yield similar correlation lengths of \(r_0 \sim \SI{0.07}{pc}\), indicating a consistent characteristic scale of the velocity fluctuations despite the different observational datasets. 
A different behavior is observed for the \n\ emission line, for which the VLT MUSE data yield \(r_0 \sim \SI{0.06}{pc}\), compared to \(r_0 \sim \SI{0.04}{pc}\) for the KPNO observations. 
More generally, the correlation length is not identical among the emission lines analyzed in this work. 
Since each line originates in a different ionization zone of the nebula, these differences may reflect variations in the characteristic scale of the turbulent motions across the ionization structure.
If this interpretation is correct, the correlation length \(r_0\) provides more than a statistical description of the velocity field; it becomes a physically meaningful quantity that can be used to probe the structure of the ionized gas.

\subsection{Comparison between MUSE and KPNO observations using the an equivalent field of view}

Here we briefly discuss the VLT MUSE results when restricted to a field of view equivalent to that of the KPNO observations. 
This analysis is motivated by the discrepancy observed in the correlation length for the \n\ emission line.

The results of this trimmed analysis are presented in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE_trim}. 
For the \n\ emission line, the correlation length decreases from \SI{0.061}{pc} for an observational box size \(L = \SI{0.64}{pc}\) to \SI{\sim 0.04}{pc} for \(L = \SI{0.45}{pc}\), bringing it into agreement with the KPNO.
In contrast, the values of \(r_0\) for the \halpha\ remain consistent and \oiii\ coincides within confidence intervals.
 
In \citet{garciav23}, we discussed the relationship between \(r_0\) and the observational box size \(L\), where the condition \(L > 10\,r_0\) was required to reliably recover \(r_0\) and our heuristic approach allows the recovery of \(r_0\) for \(L > 5\,r_0\).

For the \n\ emission line, we find \(L / r_0 \sim 10\), which would nominally satisfy this condition. 
However, the results suggest a stronger dependence between \(L\) and \(r_0\) in this case, indicating that the recovered correlation length remains sensitive to the sampled spatial extent.
This is of interest since \halpha\ and \oiii\, with a lower \(L/r_0\) value seems less affected by this difference.

A similar behavior is observed for \(\sigma\pos\), which depends on the field of view because different regions of the nebula exhibit different amplitudes of centroid velocity fluctuations. 
As discussed earlier, the outer regions are characterized by smaller amplitude fluctuations than the central parts of the nebula.
In contrast, the power-law index \(m\) appears to be largely insensitive to differences in the field of view.

These results indicate that the impact of the field of view on the recovered turbulent parameters is not uniform. 
While \(\sigma\pos\) and, in some cases, \(r_0\) remain sensitive to the spatial extent of the observations, the power-law index \(m\) appears to be more robust. 
This suggests that \(m\) provides a more reliable characterization of the turbulent cascade when comparing datasets with different spatial coverage.

\section{Summary}\label{sec:summary}

Our findings can be summarize as:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The fitting of our proposed model is enough to recover the turbulent parameters and their confidence intervals for the Orion MUSE velocity maps despite the low S/N ratio.
    \item The trade-off between noise and seeing is addressed through controlled binning, allowing the recovery of the turbulent cascade while reducing instrumental noise without significantly degrading the spatial information and reducing computational time.
    \item Weighting the structure function by the intensity and masking low-emission pixels allow the recovery of turbulent parameters when fitting the proposed model; however, unlike binning, these approaches do not lead to a significant reduction in instrumental noise.
    \item Comparison with the KPNO data yields consistent turbulent parameters, with similar power-law indices and correlation lengths, while remaining differences are attributed to the larger spatial coverage and higher spatial resolution of the VLT MUSE observations.
    \item Comparison with previous studies \citep{2019MNRAS.483..704A,2016MNRAS.455.4057M} shows that our approach provides a more consistent framework for recovering the structure of the velocity fluctuations from observational data.
    \item Each emission line exhibits a characteristic set of turbulent parameters, reflecting the physical conditions and spatial regions traced within the nebula.
    \item The parameters \(\sigma\pos\) and \(r_0\) are sensitive to the spatial extent of the observations and to large-scale velocity gradients, while the power-law index \(m\) remains largely invariant, indicating that it provides a more robust tracer of the intrinsic turbulent cascade.
\end{enumerate}
