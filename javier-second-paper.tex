% Abstract of the paper
\begin{abstract}
  Turbulence plays a critical role in the evolution of \hii\ regions, yet recovering its statistical properties from observations remains challenging, especially under low signal-to-noise conditions.
  We investigate whether high spatial resolution but low spectral resolution integral field spectroscopic observations from VLT MUSE of the Orion Nebula can recover the same turbulent velocity statistics as those derived from previously analyzed observations at high spectral resolution but lower spatial resolution.
  We analyze the centroid velocity maps by computing the second-order structure function of the velocity fluctuations in the plane-of-sky and fitting a simple model based on the
  correlation length \(r_0\), power-law slope \(m\) and noise level. 
  We systematically test the effects of spatial binning on the trade-off between instrumental noise and spatial resolution,
  using published high spectral resolution echelle spectroscopy as a point of comparison.
  We find that downsampling improves the reliability of the empirical structure function
  so long as the bin size is less than \(0.05\, r_0\),
  and that model fitting is able to recover the turbulent parameters across all binning levels. 
  Minor differences in the large-scale behavior among emission lines of different ionization potential are observed, revealing line-dependent turbulence signatures associated with the geometric and ionization structure of the nebula. 
  Despite their relatively poor velocity resolution, our methodology can turn observations from
  intermediate resolution (\(R \approx 3000\)) integral-field spectrographs such as MUSE
  into a useful tool for studying turbulent velocity, even at the subsonic velocity dispersion typical of compact \hii\ regions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

%Intro first para (what we are studying)
The process of star formation begins with the gravitational collapse of molecular clouds, followed by fragmentation into dense cores where stars form in clusters \citep{2003RPPh...66.1651L, 2007ARA&A..45..565M}. 
Massive O- and B-type stars emerge within these stellar groups, and their intense ionizing radiation and stellar winds inject substantial energy and momentum into the surrounding gas, profoundly influencing the structure and dynamics of the the interstellar medium (ISM) \citep{2006ApJ...647..397M, 2024RMxAC..58....8A}.

Observations of ionized gas reveal complex velocity patterns that deviate from simple radial expansion \citep{2025ApJ...986..159R}. ADD CITATIONS FROM FIRST ARTICLE.
Instead, these motions exhibit complex behavior that can be interpreted as turbulence. 
In this context, \hii\ regions—especially nearby ones—offer ideal laboratories for studying turbulent dynamics in the ionized ISM \citep{1987ApJ...317..686O,castaneda1988,Mivi1995,2026A&A...707A.339R}.

Turbulence in \hii\ regions is typically first characterized through the amplitude of velocity fluctuations. This can be studied using velocity dispersion along the line-of-sight (LOS) or through the variance of centroid velocities projected onto the plane-of-sky (POS) \citep{1988A&A...201..199A,arthur2016turbulence}. 
Statistical tools, such as the second-order structure function, enable the recovery of turbulence signatures in the POS such as the power-law $m$ and correlation length \(r_0\), and allow comparisons with theoretical models such as the Kolmogorov theory of incompressible turbulence \citep{von1951methode, castaneda1988, arthur2016turbulence, lagrois2011}.

However, recovering these turbulent parameters from observational data is not straightforward. 
Since the structure function characterizes how velocity fluctuations vary with spatial separation, it is essential that the measured scales reflect genuine turbulent motions rather than instrumental or environmental effects.


In practice, this condition is difficult to satisfy. 
Ground-based observations are affected by atmospheric seeing, while the smallest measurable velocity fluctuations are limited by the instrument’s spectral resolution and sensitivity. 
In addition, noise and other systematic effects can distort the observed velocity field. 
Furthermore, \hii\ regions are dynamic environments that exhibit large-scale motions not directly related to turbulence, which must be carefully disentangled when interpreting the data \citep{1979ApJ...233...85B,Mivi1995, lagrois2011}.

The Orion Nebula (M~42), located at a distance of 440 pc \citetext{\SI{1}{\arcsecond} = \SI{0.002}{pc}; \citealp{2008AJ....136.1566O}}, is the closest massive star-forming region and one of the most studied \hii\ regions. 
Ionized by the O7~V star $\theta^1$~Ori~C, the nebula exhibits complex physical and kinematic structures, including a rich population of young stars, stellar outflows, and Herbig–Haro objects \citep{1993ApJ...410..696O,2001ARA&A..39...99O,Garcia-Diaz:2007a,2009AJ....137..367O,2019MNRAS.486.3423H,2021MNRAS.502.1703M}. 
Its proximity and brightness make it a prime target for studies using spectroscopic observations \citep{Garcia-Diaz:2008a,2015A&A...582A.114W,2016ApJ...819..136A}.

Several studies have investigated turbulence in the Orion Nebula using statistical tools such as the second-order structure function \citep{von1951methode,munch1958internal,castaneda1988,1992ApJ...387..229O,arthur2016turbulence,2016MNRAS.455.4057M,2019MNRAS.483..704A}. 
However, these analyses have led to significantly different conclusions regarding the nature of the turbulent cascade. 
For example, \citet{arthur2016turbulence}, using Kitt Peak National Observatory (KPNO) echelle observations, reported clear power-law behavior consistent with a turbulent cascade, while \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}, analyzing Very Large Telescope (VLT) Multi Unit Spectroscopic Explorer (MUSE) data, found little evidence of such structure. 
More recent studies using the same VLT MUSE observations \citep{2019MNRAS.483..704A} have obtained intermediate results.

\textcolor{red}{The KPNO and MUSE datasets differ significantly in both their spectral and spatial sampling properties. 
The KPNO echelle observations have an instrumental spectral resolution of approximately \SI{8}{km.s^{-1}}, whereas the MUSE observations have a spectral resolution of about \SI{120}{km.s^{-1}} at \halpha. 
Conversely, the MUSE observations provide finer spatial sampling, with a pixel scale of \SI{0.2}{arcsec.pixel^{-1}}, compared to \(\sim1-\SI{2}{arcsec.pixel^{-1}}\) for the KPNO data. 
The combination of lower instrumental noise and higher spectral resolution makes the KPNO observations a useful reference for assessing the turbulent parameters recovered from the MUSE data.}

The discrepancies in different works are likely driven by differences in methodology, data quality, and the treatment of observational effects such as noise and spatial resolution \citep{garciav23}. 
In particular, instrumental noise can obscure small-scale velocity fluctuations, making the recovery of the turbulent cascade highly sensitive to the analysis technique. This raises a fundamental question: how can the turbulent structure function be reliably recovered from noisy integral field spectroscopic observations? 

More generally, the lack of a standardized methodology for extracting turbulent parameters from observational data makes direct comparisons between studies difficult. 
This fragmentation poses a major challenge for turbulence research, especially in the absence of a complete astrophysical turbulent theory, where observational constraints play a central role.


In \citet{garciav23}, we introduced a heuristic model for the second-order structure function and applied it to \hii\ regions of varying sizes. 
This approach provided a coherent methodology that not only enabled the recovery of turbulent parameters, but also allowed for meaningful comparisons between different regions.

In this work, we address these issues by implementing a consistent methodology to recover the turbulent structure function from noisy observations. 
By combining masking, controlled binning, and model fitting, we aim to reliably extract the turbulent parameters and provide a framework for meaningful comparison between different datasets.
We also extend its use to new observational data and address how to mitigate nuisance parameters that commonly affect the measurement of turbulence in ionized gas.

In Section~\ref{sec:observations}, we describe the VLT MUSE integral-field spectroscopic observations of the Huygens region in the Orion Nebula, which we use as a concrete example to analyze the turbulent velocity field across multiple emission lines: \halpha, \oiii, \n, \siii, and \ariii.
We also describe KPNO echelle observations which we use as reference to compare VLT MUSE results. 
%The emission line we use KPNO data are: \halpha, \oiii, \n\ and \sii.
In Section~\ref{sec:met}, we combine a systematic selection procedure with a downsampling strategy to recover the intrinsic second-order structure function of the velocity fluctuations. 
The resulting turbulent parameters are then derived and analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:results}.
In Section~\ref{sec:discussion}, we examine how the recovered turbulent parameters vary between emission lines and datasets, discuss their implications for the turbulent structure of ionized gas and compare with previous results. 
Particular attention is given to the extent to which the turbulent cascade can be recovered from noisy observational data.
Finally, Section~\ref{sec:summary} summarizes our results.

The Orion Nebula was observed with MUSE during its commissioning phase on 2014 February 16 \citep{2014Msngr.157...13B}.
A total of \(60 \times 5 \ \text{s}\) exposures were used to build a \(\SI{6}{arcmin} \times \SI{5}{arcmin}\) mosaic, in which each of the 30 distinct pointings was observed twice, employing a 90\textdegree{} dither rotation.
For details of the data reduction see \citet{2015A&A...582A.114W}.
The observations were carried out in the wide field mode with a field-of-view (FOV) of \(\SI{1}{arcmin} \times \SI{1}{arcmin}\), with a the wavelength range of \(4595-  \SI{9366}{\angstrom} \) and a sampling of \(\SI{0.2}{arcsec} \times \SI{0.2}{arcsec} \times \SI{0.85}{\angstrom}  \). 
The data is in the range of \(6300 - \SI{6800}{\angstrom}\), where the spectral resolving power is \(R \simeq 2500\) \citep{2021MNRAS.502.4597H}.
The derived velocity fields were previously analyzed in \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M} and \citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A}.

For comparison with the VLT MUSE observations, we use echelle observations of the Orion Nebula obtained with the \SI{4}{m} telescope at Kitt Peak National Observatory (KPNO), covering the \halpha, \oiii, and \n\ emission lines \citep{Doi:2004a}. 
The \sii\ data are taken from supplementary observations obtained with the Manchester Echelle Spectrometer attached to the \SI{2.1}{m} telescope at the Observatorio Astronómico Nacional at San Pedro Mártir (OAN-SPM), Mexico.
The observations map the central Huygens region of the nebula over an area of approximately \(\SI{3}{arcmin} \times \SI{5}{arcmin}\). 
The dataset is composed of 96 north-south oriented long-slit spectra, each spanning \SI{300}{arcsec}, separated by \SI{2}{arcsec}, and observed with a slit width of \SI{0.8}{arcsec}. 
The resulting velocity resolution is \SI{8}{km\,s^{-1}}. 
The centroid velocity maps from KPNO employed in this work are based on the intensity-weighted mean velocities derived by \citet{Garcia-Diaz:2008a}. 
Their analysis incorporated additional east-west slit observations to improve the relative velocity calibration between adjacent slit positions.
The derived velocity fields were previously analyzed in \citet{arthur2016turbulence}.

\textcolor{red}{Figure~\ref{fig:H_I-6563_maps} shows the two-dimensional maps of integrated intensity (left panel) and centroid velocity (right panel) for the Orion Nebula reconstructed using the MUSE data, both centered at the ionizing star of the region; the O7~V star $\theta^1$~Ori~C \citep{2006A&A...448..351S}. 
The field of view covers the brightest portion of the nebula commonly called the Huygens region.
Several well-known morphological features of the Orion Nebula can be identified in the left panel of the figure \citep{2009AJ....137..367O}. }


\textcolor{red}{The region of highest surface brightness lies southwest of the Trapezium stars and corresponds to Orion-S, an active site of star formation and stellar outflows.
Within the Trapezium, $\theta^1$~Ori~C is the main source of ionizing radiation, accompanied by three cooler B-type stars.
Toward the southeast, the Orion Bar is visible, tracing the main ionization front of the nebula. 
To the northeast, the Dark Bay appears as a region of high extinction produced by neutral material in the Veil located on the observer's side of the nebula.}

\textcolor{red}{Several prominent kinematic features can be identified in the velocity map. 
The Herbig--Haro objects such as HH~201, HH~203, and HH~204 are traced by their strongly blueshifted velocities \citep{Doi:2004a}. 
A further blueshifted structure, the Big Arc South, is located south of Orion-S. 
At redshifted velocities, the Red Bay appears northeast of this region, while the Red Fan is seen farther south \citep{Garcia-Diaz:2007a}.}

\subsection{The binning procedure}\label{sec:binning procedure}

To mitigate the impact of instrumental noise while preserving the underlying turbulent signal, we apply a spatial binning procedure to the velocity maps using the \texttt{tetrablok} Python package\footnote{\url{https://github.com/will-henney/tetrabloks}} \citep{2018MNRAS.479.3909G}. 
This approach explicitly addresses the trade-off between noise reduction and spatial resolution in the recovery of the turbulent structure function.

The binning is performed by re-sampling the original data onto progressively coarser grids using binning factors of \(2^1 = 2\), \(2^2 = 4\), \(2^3 = 8\), and \(2^4 = 16\) applied in each spatial direction.
When passing from a finer grid to the next coarser grid,
the flux-weighted average velocity is calculated over all valid pixels in a \(2 \times 2\) block,
but only if the number of valid pixels is \(\ge N_{\text{min}}\), where \(N_{\text{min}}\) is a tunable parameter.
In this paper, we adopt \(N_{\text{min}}=1\), which means that missing data on the original grid
tends to get filled in on the coarser grids.

Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_bins_comparison} shows as an example of the binned velocity maps for the \halpha\ emission line.
The original map has a size of \(1 475 \times 1765\) original pixels
(see Fig.~\ref{fig:H_I-6563_maps}) which is reduced to a size of \(91 \times 109\)
coarse pixels
through a series of four iterations.
Each iteration and the resulting velocity map is shown in the different panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:Ha_bins_comparison} for the \halpha\ emission line.

\subsection{The second-order structure function}
\label{sec:second-order-struct}

 
After the binning and masking of the maps we apply the second-order structure function, \(B(r)\), which is a function of the scalar separation or lag, \(r\),
between two points on the plane of the sky:
%
\newcommand\Abs[1]{\vert #1\vert}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Br}
  B(r) = \left\langle 
  \bigl[
  V_{c}(\xx_j) - V_{c}(\xx_i)
  \bigr]^{2} \right \rangle_{\Abs{\xx_j - \xx_i\!} \ \approx \ r} \ .
\end{equation}

The averaging is performed over all pairs of points
\((i, j)\)
whose scalar separation \(\Abs{\xx_j - \xx_i}\) is close to \(r\),
irrespective of the orientation of the separation vector.
In practice, we achieve this by binning the separations with a constant
logarithmic width of \SI{0.05}{dex}.

%We also use an intensity-weighted structure function, defined as:
%
%\begin{equation}\label{eq:Br_w}
%  B(r) =\left\langle \frac{I(\xx_j)I(\xx_i)}{\langle I(\xx_j)I(\xx_i) \rangle } 
%  \bigl[
%  V_{c}(\xx_j) - V_{c}(\xx_i)
%  \bigr]^{2}  \right \rangle_{\Abs{\xx_j - \xx_i\!} \ \approx \ r} \ .
%\end{equation}
%
%where \(I(\xx_j)\) and \(I(\xx_i)\) are the intensities %at each pair of pixels.
%In this formulation, brighter structures are emphasized, while fainter regions contribute less to the structure function, which in turn helps to mitigate the influence of noise \citep{arthur2016turbulence}.

To interpret the observed structure function and extract the underlying turbulent parameters, we adopt a parametric model that accounts for both intrinsic turbulence and observational effects \citep{garciav23}. 
In this framework, the observed structure function is modeled by combining the intrinsic structure function with the effects of atmospheric seeing, \(S(r)\), and a constant noise contribution, \(B\noise\), yielding the corrected model:
%
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{B}\model(r) = B\model(r) \,  S(r) + B\noise
  \label{eq:sf-functional}
\end{equation}
where \(B\model(r)\) represents the intrinsic (noise- and seeing-free) structure function, which we model as:
%
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal}
  B\model(r) = 2\sigma^2\pos \left[
    1 - 2^{- \left( r/r_0 \right)^m} \ .
  \right]
\end{equation}
%
Here, \(r_0\) is the correlation length, the characteristic length of the velocity fluctuations, and \(m\) is the is the power-law index, which characterize the slope of the turbulent cascade.
The model in equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal} corresponds to the \textit{stable covariance model}, a standard model used in the field of geostatistics \citep{wackernagel2003multivariate}.
%This behavior closely resembles the observed structure functions analyzed in this work.

In equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal}, the seeing \(S(r)\) is given by:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ffs}
  S(r) =
  \left[ \frac{1}{1 + 1.25 s_0 / r_0} \right]
  \left[ \frac{1}{1 + (2.6 s_0 / r)^{1.5}} \right]
  ,
\end{equation}
where \(s_0\) is the RMS width of the seeing profile\footnote{%
  Note that the full-width half maximum (FWHM) seeing width is
  \(2 (2 \ln 2)^{1/2} s_0 \approx 2.35 s_0\).
}.

The observed structure function, \(B\obs(r)\), is computed using equation~\eqref{eq:Br}. 
The uncertainties in \(B\obs(r)\) are assumed to be dominated by systematic effects rather than random noise. Consequently, we assign an uncertainty to each bin equal to a constant fraction (1–3\%) of the observed value (see column~3 of Table~\ref{tab:fitting-parameters}).

Model fitting is performed using a non-linear weighted least-squares approach based on the Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm \citep{More:1978a}, as implemented in the \texttt{lmfit} Python library \citep{newville_matthew_2014_11813}. 
The posterior distributions of the model parameters are estimated using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) ensemble sampling \citep{2010CAMCS...5...65G}, implemented in the \texttt{emcee} Python library \citep{2013PASP..125..306F}.  
Table~\ref{tab:parameter-ranges} lists the uniform prior distributions assumed for each parameter.

To assess the reliability of the fits, we consider the turbulent parameters to be reliably recovered when a power-law regime is identified and the derived parameters are well constrained. For this, we require statistical consistency between the model and the data, quantified by a reduced chi-square value \(\chi^2 \lesssim 1\) (see column~7 of Table~\ref{tab:fitting-parameters}). 
We also verify that the posterior distributions of the fitted parameters are well-behaved, without significant asymmetries or multimodalities, within the 95\% confidence interval.

A detailed description of our structure function methodology can be found in \citet{garciav23}. 
In this work, we focus on its application to observational data processed through binning and masking, with the aim of recovering the underlying turbulent cascade.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

In this section we present the turbulent parameters and their confidence intervals obtained by fitting equation~\eqref{eq:sf-functional} to the observed structure function, \(B\obs(r)\). 

The results are analyzed to assess how observational effects, such as noise, spatial resolution, and field of view, as well as different analysis methods, impact the recovery of the structure function and the derived parameters.

\subsection{Intensity vs radial velocity diagrams}\label{sec:sb vs velocity diagrams}

Figure~\ref{fig:intensity_velocity} shows the joint distribution histograms of surface brightness and centroid velocity
for the three emission lines \n{}, \halpha{}, \oiii{} from both the VLT MUSE and KPNO datasets. 
The three rows in the figure correspond to the full VLT MUSE field of view,
the VLT MUSE data restricted to the KPNO field of view, and the KPNO observations, respectively.
These diagrams provide a direct view of the relation between line brightness and centroid velocity for each emission line, allowing us to compare the velocity distribution of bright and faint gas across datasets.
Visual comparison of the bottom two rows shows a remarkable consistency between the MUSE and KPNO
results for each emission line, when restricted to the same field of view.
Fine details in the joint distributions can be matched between the two datasets, but there are two main differences,
which indicate the limitations of the data.
First, there is a systematic offset between the MUSE and KPNO velocities of \qtyrange{3}{6}{km.s^{-1}},
which is not unexpected given the finding by \citet{2015A&A...582A.114W} of a similar offset between MUSE velocities
and values from the literature \citep{Baldwin:2000a}.
The precision of the KPNO absolute velocities is estimated to be better than this, at \(\approx \qty{2}{km.s^{-1}}\) \citep{Garcia-Diaz:2008a}.
Second, the KPNO distributions appear to be slightly compressed along the brightness axis,
compared with the MUSE distributions.
This is probably due to the fact that the MUSE spectra are sky-subtracted,
while the KPNO spectra are not,
with the resultant difference in the brightness zero point manifesting
as stretching/compression on a log scale.
Since this paper concentrates on relative velocity fluctuations and employs the surface brightness
only for masking the velocity maps,
our results are not significantly affected by either of these issues.

A third difference is that the MUSE joint distribution histograms in Figure~\ref{fig:intensity_velocity}
appear more blurred than the KPNO ones, especially along the velocity axis,
which is a direct result of the larger uncertainty in the radial velocity measurements of the MUSE observations.
This can be improved by increasing the binning level of the maps,
but only at the cost of worsening the counting-statistics fluctuations of the histograms
due to the reduced number of map pixels.

\subsubsection{Representative case study of the \halpha\ line}
\label{sec:Representative case study}


% Present Brs for Halpha
The solid markers of different colors in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison} indicate the results of applying equation~\eqref{eq:Br} to the non-binned \halpha\ map shown in Figure~\ref{fig:velocity_maps_shared_cbar_fixed}, and the corresponding binned maps in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_bins_comparison}.
The dotted curves correspond to the heuristic model described in equation~\eqref{eq:sf-functional}, while the dashed curves represent the ideal model given in equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal}.  
For each binning level the size of the pixel in parsecs is \(\binsize = 0.0004\), \num{0.0008}, \num{0.0016}, \num{0.0032} and \(0.00064\), respectively.

% Present trubulent parameters for Halpha
For the \halpha\ emission we were able to fit the model to all the observed structure functions. Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE_Ha} lists the best-fit values for the model parameters with 95\% confidence interval.
The first column corresponds to the value for the binning size used in each map before computing the structure function.
The nuisance values like the noise and seeing (in RMS) are given in columns three and five.
The turbulent parameters are listed in columns two, six and eight.
Finally, columns nine and ten list the quotients \(s_0/r_0\) and \(\binsize{} / r_0\), which are used for a sanity check of the results.
We also estimate the signal to noise ratio, SNR, of the turbulent fluctuations as:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:SNR}
  \text{SNR} = \frac{\sigma\pos}{\sigma_\text{noise}} = \left( \frac{2 \sigma\pos^2}{B_\text{noise}} \right)^{0.5}.
\end{equation}

% Halpha Discussion small scales
For the non-binned \halpha\ map, a power-law trend is present, but the structure at small scales is significantly affected by instrumental noise, with a derived value of \SI{2.61}{km^{2}.s^{-2}}.  
The structure functions derived from the binned maps are also shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison}.  
As the bin size increases, from \(2\times2\) to \(16\times16\), the small-scale structure of the velocity field becomes progressively clearer, as evidenced by the emergence of a well-defined power-law behavior and a reduction of the instrumental noise to a value of \SI{0.39}{km^{2}.s^{-2}}.  
% Noise reduction
For per-pixel uncorrelated noise, the variance of the rebinned map is expected to decrease as \(N_{\mathrm{pix}}^{-1}\), where \(N_{\mathrm{pix}}\) is the number of pixels within the bin. 
However, the observed decline in \(B_\text{noise}\) is less steep than this, indicating that the ``noise'' is partially spatially correlated and dominated by systematic instrumental effects rather than Poisson statistics.
%Instead, part of the noise must be spatially correlated, likely reflecting the observational and reconstruction processes involved in the MUSE data.

At the binning level of \(8 \times 8\) and above, the binning begins to remove physically relevant small-scale structure.
In the model fits, this is manifested as an increase in the \(s_0\) parameter
(Fig.~\ref{fig:parameters vs binning}),
which was designed to model atmospheric seeing but in this case is responding to the effective smoothing induced by the binning.
Above the same binning level, the derived noise also stops decreasing, indicating
that \(4 \times 4\) is the optimum binning level for this dataset,
which corresponds to \(\binsize{} < 0.05 r_0\).

% Halpha Large scales
At large spatial scales, the \halpha\ structure function shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison} exhibits a downward hook, which reflects a reduction in the amplitude of the velocity fluctuations towards the edges of the observational field. 

% Model recovering for all maps
An important result shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison} is that the fitting procedure of the equation~\eqref{eq:sf-functional}, is able to recover the turbulent parameters even at low signal-to-noise ratios. 
This is illustrated by the dashed lines, which represent the ideal model given by equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal} and closely match the observed structure functions across different binning levels. 

This demonstrates that our methodology, based on fitting a heuristic model to the observational data, is sufficient to recover the turbulent parameters of the velocity field. 
In practice, this implies that either the non-binned maps can be used, at the expense of increased computational cost, or binned maps can be employed within the constraint imposed by the ratio \(\binsize{} / r_0\), without compromising the recovery of the turbulent cascade. 
This result extends the applicability of the method to datasets with low signal-to-noise ratios or limited spatial resolution.

\subsubsection{Representative case study of the \ariii\ emission line}\label{sec:Representative case study Ar}

% [Ar III] Discussion
Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE_Ar} presents the best-fit model parameters and their corresponding confidence intervals for the \ariii\ emission line, where the columns represent the same quantities as in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE_Ha}. The associated structure functions are shown in the right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:Ha_Br_comparison}.

As discussed for the \halpha\ emission, the heuristic model given by equation~\eqref{eq:sf-functional} provides a good fit to all observed structure functions. 
This result demonstrates that the combined use of binning and model fitting allows the turbulent cascade to be reliably recovered even in low signal-to-noise conditions. 
In particular, it highlights the robustness of the methodology when applied to fainter emission lines, where noise effects are more significant.

The \ariii\ emission exhibits a similar trend to that observed for \halpha, in which the structure of the velocity fluctuations can be recovered either by fitting the model to the non-binned map or by applying spatial binning. 
Since this line is intrinsically fainter, the binning procedure is particularly effective at recovering the velocity structure, reducing the instrumental noise from \SI{10.8}{km^{2}.s^{-2}} to \SI{1}{km^{2}.s^{-2}}.

One key difference for the \ariii\ emission, compared to \halpha\ in our analysis, is the behavior of the inferred seeing parameter \(s_0\), which decreases from the non-binned map and reaches a minimum value of \SI{1.4}{arcsec} at intermediate binning levels (bin~4). 
We attribute this behavior to the way the model responds to different noise regimes. For the non-binned (bin~0) and lightly binned (bin~1) maps, the signal-to-noise ratio is low due to the faintness of the line, and the observed structure function does not exhibit a clear power-law behavior, as it is dominated by noise. 
In this regime, the fitting procedure compensates by adopting relatively large values of \(s_0\), effectively reproducing the apparent flattening of the structure function at small separations.

As the noise is reduced through binning (e.g., bin~2), the underlying power-law behavior becomes more clearly defined, allowing the model to recover smaller and more physically meaningful values of \(s_0\), consistent with the presence of resolved small-scale velocity fluctuations.
At larger binning factors, however, the averaging process begins to smooth out these small-scale fluctuations, leading again to an increase in the inferred \(s_0\). 
In this regime, the rise in \(s_0\) reflects the loss of intrinsic small-scale structure due to the effective blurring introduced by the binning procedure.

Regarding the large-scale behavior of the \ariii\ structure functions, we observe a transition from a periodic, oscillatory pattern in the non-binned map to a radial-gradient behavior in the binned maps, similar to that found for \halpha.  
This suggests that the oscillatory pattern is likely driven by the low signal-to-noise ratio in the non-binned data, while the smoothing introduced by binning recovers the intrinsic large-scale velocity structure of the emission line.

As in the case of \halpha, the observed structure functions show good overall agreement with the model described by equation~\eqref{eq:model-strucfunc-ideal} across all binned samples.
This behavior further supports the robustness of the methodology in recovering the turbulent structure across different emission lines, even under varying signal-to-noise conditions.

%The absence of a standardized methodology for determining the power-law index complicates direct comparisons between different studies. 
%The model adopted in this work provides a consistent framework for fitting observed structure functions and extracting the parameters that describe the turbulent velocity fluctuations, offering a robust basis for future inter-dataset comparisons.
An additional advantage of our methodology is that the model fitting also allows us to recover the correlation length \(r_0\), which represents a characteristic scale of the turbulent structure. 
For the \halpha\, \oiii\ and \siii\ emission lines the VLT MUSE data yield similar correlation lengths of \(r_0 \sim \SI{0.07}{pc}\).
A different behavior is observed for the \n\ and \ariii| emission lines, for which data yield \(r_0 \sim \SI{0.06}{pc}\). 

% Power-law discussion
The values listed in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE} indicate that the power-law index is broadly consistent for the \halpha, \oiii, \siii, and \ariii\ emission lines, with \(m \sim \num{1.1}\). 
The \n\ emission line represents the main exception, showing a slightly shallower slope with \(m \sim \num{0.9}\).

Since each line originates in a different ionization zone of the nebula, these differences may reflect variations in the characteristic scale of the turbulent motions across the ionization structure.
If this interpretation is correct, the correlation length \(r_0\) and the power-law index \(m\) provides more than a statistical description of the velocity field; it becomes a physically meaningful quantity that can be used to probe the structure of the ionized gas.

\subsection{Comparison with previous works}

We compare our results with recent studies that have computed the second-order structure function for the Orion Nebula. 
In particular, \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M} and \citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A} analyzed the same VLT/MUSE observations used in this work, while \citet{arthur2016turbulence} investigated the structure function using KPNO echelle observations.

\subsubsection{Comparison with \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}}\label{sec:comp_McLeod}

\citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M} computed the second-order structure function following the method of \citet{2015MNRAS.447.1341B} for a randomly selected sample of \(10^3\) pixels \(j\), around which they radially bin all other pixels \(i\). 
They applied an intensity-based selection criterion for a mask, excluding pixels with \oi\ surface brightness below \SI{\sim 1e-16}{erg.s^{-1}.cm^{-2}.pixel^{-1}},
then computed the second-order structure function for both the masked and unmasked velocity maps, while also considering the influence of high-velocity objects.

In their analysis, the power-law index was estimated by fitting a straight line to the observed structure function over a restricted range (\(\approx \qty{0.7}{dex}\))
of spatial separations.
For the highest S/N lines of \halpha{} and \oiii{} they determined indices in the range
\(m = \numrange{0.22}{0.45}\),
as compared with the value \(m = 1.1\) that we find for the same two lines (Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE}).

They argue that the structure function cannot be reliably recovered due to lower spectral resolution, short exposure times and a low S/N ratio. 
However, as demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:met}, the recovery of the structure function is strongly dependent on the analysis methodology, and appropriate treatment of noise and spatial resolution allows the turbulent signal to be recovered even in low signal-to-noise conditions.
Inspection of \citeauthor{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}'s measured \halpha{} structure function (their Fig.~12a)
reveals it to be closely similar to our own (our Fig.~\ref{fig:fit_results_01}a)
over the same spatial range, once the difference in normalizations is accounted for.
As recognized by \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M}, the shallow apparent slopes that they find are 
entirely due to not correcting for the effects of noise.


\subsubsection{Comparison with \citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A} }\label{sec:comp_Anorve}

\citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A} compute the second-order structure function for the \halpha\ emission line using both a real space algorithm and the standard Fourier space algorithm.
The structure function is computed for both the raw maps and a set of filtered maps. 
The raw map include the full velocity range of the PDF, defined. 
The filtered maps are then constructed by selecting narrower intervals from this distribution using some asymmetric velocity cutoffs: \(-9\sigma < v < 5\sigma\), \(-1\sigma < v < 5\sigma\), \(-9\sigma < v < -1\sigma\), and \(-1\sigma < v < 1\sigma\). 
This different filters are named as filter~1, filter~2 and so on.

The raw map corresponds to the same dataset as the unmasked map of \citet{2016MNRAS.455.4057M} which consider the complete region.
Filter~2 and filter~4 corresponds to a representation of the mean field eliminating high velocity blue-shifted pixels while in filter~4 the Big Arc South, Big Fan, Big Bay, and the Bright Bar are masked.
Filter~3 corresponds to high velocity objects which are blue-shifted but their kinematics does not represent the characteristic velocity fluctuations on the region.

For their filter~1 the values of the mean velocity and dispersion are \SI{14.39}{km.s^{-1}} and \SI{3.72}{km.s^{-1}}. 
For their filter~2 the values are \SI{15.11}{km.s^{-1}} and \SI{2.80}{km.s^{-1}}.
For their filter~3 the values are \SI{6.65}{km.s^{-1}} and \SI{3.70}{km.s^{-1}}.
And finally, for their filter~4 the values are \SI{14.53}{km.s^{-1}} and \SI{2.27}{km.s^{-1}}.
As comparison our velocity statistics give us \SI{13.85}{km.s^{-1}} and \SI{2.82}{km.s^{-1}}, making filter~2 the most accurate comparison.

\citet{2019MNRAS.483..704A} fitted the structure function with a power-law function of the form \(a x^b\) over the spatial range of \(0.129 < r < 0.645\ \text{pc}\). 
They report values of \(b \sim \num{0.73}\) for the raw data and \(b \sim \num{0.77}\) for filter~1. 
For filter~2, they obtain \(b \sim \num{0.68}\), whereas for filter~4 they find power-law index of \(b=\num{0.5566}\). 
In comparison our results for the \halpha\ emission line is \num{1.1}.

\subsubsection{Comparison with \citet{arthur2016turbulence} }\label{sec:comp_Arthur}

\citet{arthur2016turbulence} derived the structure function using echelle observations from the Kitt Peak National Observatory (KPNO) for \halpha, \oiii, and \n, complemented by data from the Observatorio Astronómico Nacional at San Pedro Mártir (OAN-SPM) for the \sii\ line.
In their work a mask is constructed from pixels with extreme values of the centroid identified through consideration of their PDFs. 
These pixels correspond to high-velocity features, such as HH 201, 202, 203, 204, and 529, and also to numerical artifacts introduced during the construction of the velocity maps from the individual slit observations.

For direct comparison, Table~\ref{tab:results_KPNO} lists the turbulent parameters obtained by applying our methodology to the KPNO data and in Appendix~\ref{apex:kpno_structure_function} we present the structure functions and their corresponding corner plots and the fitting parameters in Table~\ref{tab:fitting-parameters-KPNO}. 

Since the KPNO observations cover an area of \(3' \times 5'\), while the VLT MUSE observations cover \(6' \times 5'\) (both centered on $\theta^1$~Orionis~C), one key difference seen in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE} and Table~\ref{tab:results_KPNO} is the larger value of \(\sigma\pos\) measured for the \halpha\ and \oiii\ emission lines in the KPNO data. 

Because the \halpha\ emission traces a larger volume of the nebula, whereas the \oiii\ emission originates primarily from the inner regions, the observed difference is likely related to the spatial extent of the observations. 
The larger MUSE field of view samples both the central high-amplitude fluctuations and the more quiescent outer regions, where the amplitude of the centroid velocity fluctuations is lower. Consequently, the measured value of \(\sigma\pos^2\) differs from that obtained using the smaller KPNO field of view.

For the power-law index \(m\), the values derived from the VLT MUSE and KPNO datasets are broadly consistent. 
As reported in \citet{arthur2016turbulence}, the more pronounced steepening below \SI{2}{arcsec} (\SI{\sim0.002}{pc}) in the KPNO observations arises from the spatial resolution of the data, corresponding to the regime where seeing effects and the inter-slit separation become significant (See section~3.2.1 of their work). 
This behavior is reflected in the fitting of our model, which yields slightly higher values of \(m\) for the KPNO data. 
This can be seen in the observed structure functions (compare solid blue markers in Figure~\ref{fig:fit_results_01} and Figure~\ref{fig:fit_results_04}), which lie below the ideal model (green curves in the figures), in contrast to the VLT MUSE observations which lie above the model.

Values reported by \citet{arthur2016turbulence} are \(m>1\) for \halpha\ and \oiii\ emission lines and \(m\sim1\) for \n\ and \sii\ emission lines which matches our results in Table~\ref{tab:results_KPNO}.
The power-law indices were determined through fitting a line between scales of \(0.016 < r < 0.044 \ \text{pc}\) for all emission lines except \sii\ which was obtained for scales  \(0.014 < r < 0.064 \ \text{pc}\).

\subsection{Comparison between MUSE and KPNO observations using the an equivalent field of view}

Here we briefly discuss the VLT MUSE results when restricted to a field of view equivalent to that of the KPNO observations. 
This analysis is motivated by the discrepancy observed in the correlation length for the \n\ emission line.

The results of this trimmed analysis are presented in Table~\ref{tab:results_MUSE_trim}. 
For the \n\ emission line, the correlation length decreases from \SI{0.061}{pc} for an observational box size \(L = \SI{0.64}{pc}\) to \SI{\sim 0.04}{pc} for \(L = \SI{0.45}{pc}\), bringing it into agreement with the KPNO.
In contrast, the values of \(r_0\) for the \halpha\ remain consistent and \oiii\ coincides within confidence intervals.
 
In \citet{garciav23}, we discussed the relationship between \(r_0\) and the observational box size \(L\), where the condition \(L > 10\,r_0\) was required to reliably recover \(r_0\) and our heuristic approach allows the recovery of \(r_0\) for \(L > 5\,r_0\).

For the \n\ emission line, we find \(L / r_0 \sim 10\), which would nominally satisfy this condition. 
However, the results suggest a stronger dependence between \(L\) and \(r_0\) in this case, indicating that the recovered correlation length remains sensitive to the sampled spatial extent.
This is of interest since \halpha\ and \oiii\, with a lower \(L/r_0\) value seems less affected by this difference.

A similar behavior is observed for \(\sigma\pos\), which depends on the field of view because different regions of the nebula exhibit different amplitudes of centroid velocity fluctuations. 
As discussed earlier, the outer regions are characterized by smaller amplitude fluctuations than the central parts of the nebula.
In contrast, the power-law index \(m\) appears to be largely insensitive to differences in the field of view.

These results indicate that the impact of the field of view on the recovered turbulent parameters is not uniform. 
While \(\sigma\pos\) and, in some cases, \(r_0\) remain sensitive to the spatial extent of the observations, the power-law index \(m\) appears to be more robust. 
This suggests that \(m\) provides a more reliable characterization of the turbulent cascade when comparing datasets with different spatial coverage.


\section{Summary}\label{sec:summary}

Our findings can be summarize as:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The fitting of our proposed model is enough to recover the turbulent parameters and their confidence intervals for the Orion MUSE velocity maps despite the low S/N ratio.
    \item The trade-off between noise and seeing is addressed through controlled binning, allowing the recovery of the turbulent cascade while reducing instrumental noise without significantly degrading the spatial information and reducing computational time.
  %  \item Weighting the structure function by the intensity and masking low-emission pixels allow the recovery of turbulent parameters when fitting the proposed model; however, unlike binning, these approaches do not lead to a significant reduction in instrumental noise.
    \item Comparison with the KPNO data yields consistent turbulent parameters, with similar power-law indices and correlation lengths, while remaining differences are attributed to the larger spatial coverage and higher spatial resolution of the VLT MUSE observations.
    \item Comparison with previous studies \citep{2019MNRAS.483..704A,2016MNRAS.455.4057M} shows that our approach provides a more consistent framework for recovering the structure of the velocity fluctuations from observational data.
    \item Each emission line exhibits a characteristic set of turbulent parameters, reflecting the physical conditions and spatial regions traced within the nebula.
    \item The parameters \(\sigma\pos\) and \(r_0\) are sensitive to the spatial extent of the observations and to large-scale velocity gradients, while the power-law index \(m\) remains largely invariant, indicating that it provides a more robust tracer of the intrinsic turbulent cascade.
\end{enumerate}
